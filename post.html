Pacific Asia in 1 month

Smiles, Bumps, Friends, and Memories for a Lifetime

Posted by Forrest Smith on June 8, 2025

I’ve always loved airplanes—airports, boarding passes, the rhythm of takeoff and landing. So spending a month flying through New Zealand, Australia, Thailand, Vietnam, Taiwan, and Japan felt like the most natural way to see the world for me. One flight blurred into the next, and before I knew it, whole countries were turning into memories instead of pins on a map.

New Zealand was where everything first felt unreal. The glow worm caves were quiet and surreal, floating through darkness beneath what looked like a living galaxy. But Hobbiton might have surprised me even more. Walking through those green hills, round doors, and tiny details felt less like a movie set and more like stepping into someone else’s peaceful little world. New Zealand had a calm to it that settled into me right away.

Australia pulled the energy back up. The Sydney Opera House didn’t disappoint—it was bold and striking, sitting right against the water like it belonged there. What stuck with me most, though, was how the city moved: ferries crossing the harbor, people packed along the shoreline, life happening outdoors constantly. It felt alive without feeling stressful.

Thailand shifted the entire mood of the trip. Bangkok was loud, hot, crowded, and never seemed to slow down—and I loved it for that. The Royal Palace felt endless in gold and detail. One of my favorite days was riding a boat along the west side of the city on the river, drifting past everyday life along the water before arriving at the massive reclining Buddha. Seeing it in person was overwhelming in scale and quiet in feeling.

And then there was the food. Thailand completely reset my expectations. Every meal felt unbeatable—street food especially. Eating off plastic stools late at night with traffic flying past became normal, and somehow every dish still tasted incredible. Pad Thai, mango sticky rice, grilled meat, fresh fruit—Thailand made food an experience, not just a necessity.

The Final Frontier

Chiang Mai slowed everything back down. Bathing elephants wasn’t flashy or rushed—it was calm and personal. Standing in the water, brushing mud off animals that carried so much quiet strength, I felt fully present. No crowds. No noise. Just one of those moments that doesn’t need explanation to feel meaningful.

Vietnam felt like controlled chaos wrapped in beauty. Tam Coc was calm and unreal, drifting between towering green cliffs while someone rowed from behind. Lan Ha Bay felt open and endless, like the trip paused for a breath. But Hanoi was the opposite—crossing the streets there felt like stepping into moving traffic and trusting the flow to catch you. The energy was intense, busy, loud, and completely unforgettable.

Wisdom is gained through experiance, not text books. Find time to find the world and you will find yourself and how you fit in.

Then came Taiwan—the place that surprised me the most and might be my favorite overall. The food there was unreal: night markets, dumplings, noodles, and dishes I still think about. Hiking Elephant Mountain gave me one of the best city views I’ve ever seen, and Taipei 101 made the skyline feel massive from above. But what really stood out was the metro—easy, efficient, spotless, and almost completely silent. It made the whole city feel effortless to move through.

This is the way life is ment to be

Japan was powerful in a very different way. Himeji Castle was sharp, white, and historic in the most classic sense. Hiroshima, though, left the deepest mark. It was quiet, heavy, and important. Visiting the gate over the water at Miyajima felt peaceful—like standing in a place that quietly holds both sorrow and hope at once. That day stayed with me.

To go places and do things that have never been done before – that’s what living is all about.

Tokyo, surprisingly, didn’t fully click for me. It was impressive, fast, neon-lit, and overwhelming in scale—but it never quite felt like mine. Riding the bullet train was still unreal, watching the countryside blur past at impossible speed. Even when a place isn’t your favorite, it still adds a layer to how you understand the world.

By the time I flew home, everything felt stitched together by time zones and boarding passes. I didn’t just visit places—I felt their pace, their silence, their chaos, their flavor. A month across six countries didn’t give me answers, but it gave me perspective, confidence, and a deeper love for movement itself. And fittingly, some of my favorite moments of the whole trip happened thousands of feet in the air.
